# BhashaSethu — Project Notes
### "Language Bridge" — A translator app for travellers in India

---

## What is this app?

I want to make an app for people from Delhi (or anywhere in North India) who travel to Bangalore and cannot speak Kannada. Most locals in Bangalore — autorickshaw drivers, shopkeepers, maids, plumbers — do not speak Hindi or English fluently. So a tourist or a new resident feels completely lost trying to communicate simple things like "how much does this cost?" or "please fix the tap."

The app is called **BhashaSethu**, which means "Language Bridge" in Kannada.

The idea is simple: you open the app, speak in Hindi, and the app speaks back in Kannada for the other person to hear. Like a live interpreter in your pocket. No internet required for common phrases. Works on any Android or iPhone without needing to install anything — just open in browser and use.

---

## Who is it for?

- A person from Delhi visiting Bangalore for work, tourism, or moving there permanently
- Someone who wants to talk to their maid, cook, security guard, plumber, or electrician but cannot understand each other
- Tourists who need help in cabs, shops, restaurants, hotels
- Anyone who needs to handle a small emergency and cannot explain themselves

---

## What should the app do?

There are three main things the app should do:

### 1. Live Voice Translator (main feature)

- You press a button and speak in your language (say, Hindi)
- The app listens, understands what you said, and converts it to Kannada (or whichever language you choose)
- It then reads out the Kannada translation loudly so the other person can hear
- You can also switch sides — so the other person can speak in Kannada and it translates to Hindi for you
- There should be a small animation showing the app is listening (like sound waves)

### 2. Phrasebook (for common situations)

- A ready-made list of useful phrases, organised by situation
- For example: things you say to a cab driver, things you say at a shop, things at a restaurant, things at a hotel, things in an emergency
- Also: things you say to a maid, cook, security guard, plumber, electrician — because these situations happen every day for someone new to the city
- For each phrase, you can tap a button and the app will speak it out loud in the other language
- You can also search for a specific phrase
- The phrases should already be translated — no waiting, no internet needed for common ones

### 3. Settings (personalise the app)

- Choose which language you speak (source) and which language you want to translate to (target)
- Support for 11 Indian languages: Hindi, Kannada, Tamil, Telugu, Malayalam, Marathi, Gujarati, Punjabi, Bengali, Odia, English
- Choose the voice of the speaker (male or female, different accents)
- Switch between light mode and dark mode (for night use)
- Save your name, city, and a small profile picture (just an emoji)

---

## How should it look?

Think of a clean, simple mobile app — like the kind you see on an iPhone. Everything should be big and easy to tap. The design should feel calm and trustworthy, not cluttered.

**Colours:**
- Main accent colour: WhatsApp green but lighter
- Background: white in day mode, dark black in night mode
- Text: dark in day mode, white in night mode

**Layout:**
- At the bottom of every screen, there are three buttons: Translate, Phrases, Settings
- Each screen has a title at the top
- Fonts should be clean and modern — easy to read

**The four screens:**

1. **Home screen** — A welcoming screen with the app name, a short description, quick buttons to start translating or browse phrases, and your profile emoji at the top

2. **Translate screen** — A big microphone button in the centre. When you press it, it listens and shows a sound wave animation. After listening, it shows what you said and then the translation below it. There's a speaker button to play the translation out loud.

3. **Phrasebook screen** — A grid of category tiles (cab, food, hotel, emergency, maid, etc.) with a colourful emoji icon. Tap a category to see the phrases inside. Tap the speaker icon on any phrase to hear it. There's a search bar to find any phrase quickly.

4. **Settings screen** — Clean sections for: your profile, appearance (light/dark), source language, target language, and voice selection.

---

## What languages should it support?

The app should work for people speaking any of these 11 Indian languages, in any combination:

1. Hindi
2. Kannada
3. Tamil
4. Telugu
5. Malayalam
6. Marathi
7. Gujarati
8. Punjabi
9. Bengali
10. Odia
11. English

So a Tamil speaker in Bangalore can also use this. Or a Gujarati tourist in Chennai.

---

## What phrases should be in the phrasebook?

There should be around 60 ready-made phrases, divided into 10 situations:

**Cab / Rickshaw** — e.g. "Please take me to this address", "How much will it cost?", "Stop here please", "Can you go faster?", "Do you know this place?", "Please use the meter"

**Shopping** — e.g. "How much does this cost?", "Can I get a discount?", "Do you have a smaller size?", "I will take this", "Do you accept card?", "Can I return this?"

**Food & Dining** — e.g. "I am vegetarian", "Is this spicy?", "The bill please", "No onion no garlic", "Water please", "This is very tasty"

**Hotel & Stay** — e.g. "I have a booking", "Can I get a late checkout?", "The AC is not working", "Please clean the room", "I need extra towels", "What is the WiFi password?"

**Emergency** — e.g. "Please call an ambulance", "I need a doctor", "Call the police", "I am lost", "I need help", "Where is the nearest hospital?"

**Household Help (Maid)** — e.g. "Please mop the floor", "Clean the bathroom", "Do the dishes", "Do not touch my things", "Come tomorrow at 9am", "Please do the laundry"

**Cook** — e.g. "Please make dal and rice", "Less oil please", "Do not add chilli", "Is lunch ready?", "Please come early tomorrow", "This tastes great"

**Security Guard** — e.g. "Do not let anyone in without calling me", "Note down the visitors", "My guest is coming, let them in", "Close the gate at night", "Has anyone asked for me?", "Please call a cab for me"

**Plumber** — e.g. "The tap is leaking", "The toilet is blocked", "There is no water", "Fix it today please", "How much will you charge?", "When will you come?"

**Electrician** — e.g. "There is no power in this room", "The fan is not working", "Fix the switchboard", "There is a short circuit", "Come as soon as possible", "How long will the repair take?"

Each phrase should be already translated into all 11 languages — so the app works instantly without needing to connect to the internet for common phrases.

---

## What should the app remember?

- Your chosen languages (source and target)
- Your preferred voice
- Your light/dark mode choice
- Your name, city, and profile emoji

It should remember all of this even if you close the app and come back later.

---

## Should it work offline?

For the phrasebook — yes, mostly. The phrases and their translations are already built into the app. The audio (speaking the phrase out loud) may need internet the first time, but after that it should remember and work offline.

For live voice translation — it needs internet, because it has to understand your speech and translate it. But it should be fast and smooth.

---

## What kind of device should it work on?

- Any smartphone — Android or iPhone
- Should work in the browser, no need to install anything from the app store
- But it should also be installable — like when Chrome/Safari says "Add to Home Screen" — and then it feels like a real app
- The whole app should be designed for a phone screen, not a computer

---

## What should it NOT do?

- It should not store your voice recordings anywhere
- It should not require sign-up or login
- It should not be slow or complicated
- It should not show ads
- It should not share your data with anyone

---

## Nice-to-have ideas (future)

- A history tab showing your recent translations
- Favourite phrases you can bookmark
- Sharing a phrase via WhatsApp
- A camera feature to translate text in a photo (like a menu or sign)
- Offline voice recognition for common commands

---

## Summary in one paragraph

BhashaSethu is a simple, beautiful mobile app that helps Hindi-speaking people communicate with Kannada-speaking locals in Bangalore — and more broadly, any two people in India who speak different languages. You either speak into it and hear the translation spoken back, or you browse a ready-made list of common phrases for everyday situations. It remembers your settings, works mostly offline for common phrases, and feels like a native app even though you open it in a browser. It's designed for real situations: the morning conversation with your maid, the argument with an auto driver about the fare, or the panic of a medical emergency in an unfamiliar city.
